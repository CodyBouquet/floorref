Carpet Colorfastness Explained Carpet · Lightfastness · Ozone Fastness · Crocking · Dye Method · AATCC Rating Scales

Colorfastness describes how well carpet retains its color when exposed to sunlight, cleaning agents, atmospheric pollutants, and rubbing contact. Multiple types of colorfastness are tested separately — a carpet with excellent lightfastness may still have weaker ozone resistance, or vice versa. Dye method is the most important determinant of overall colorfastness. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Colorfastness covers multiple color stability tests: lightfastness (sunlight), ozone fastness (atmospheric), and crocking (color transfer by rubbing). Solution-dyed fiber provides the best colorfastness across all categories because color is part of the fiber structure. Ratings are reported on scales such as the AATCC Gray Scale (1–5, where 5 = no change) or Blue Wool scale. High ratings reduce — but do not eliminate — fading risk.

Types of Colorfastness

Lightfastness

Lightfastness measures resistance to color change from light exposure — primarily UV radiation from sunlight. It is tested by exposing carpet samples to controlled xenon arc or carbon arc light sources for defined periods and measuring color change against a reference. Test methods include AATCC 16 (Colorfastness to Light). Results are reported on the AATCC Gray Scale (1–5) or on the Blue Wool Scale (1–8). Lightfastness is most relevant for carpets installed near windows, in sun rooms, or in any location with prolonged direct or indirect sunlight.

Ozone fastness measures resistance to color change from atmospheric ozone and other pollutants. Some nylon carpets are susceptible to ozone bleaching — a yellowing or color shift caused by oxidizing atmospheric pollutants, not by light exposure. This is tested per AATCC 129. High ozone fastness is particularly important in outdoor-adjacent or industrial environments with elevated atmospheric pollutant levels.

Crocking tests measure color transfer from the carpet to other surfaces by rubbing contact. Dry crocking measures transfer under dry conditions; wet crocking measures transfer when the carpet is wet. Both are tested per AATCC 8 and rated on the AATCC Gray Scale for staining (1–5, where 5 = no transfer). Crocking matters in applications where light-colored clothing, furniture upholstery, or walls are in direct contact with carpet edges or pile.

How Dye Method Affects Colorfastness

Dye method is the primary determinant of colorfastness because it determines how deeply and permanently color is anchored to the fiber. Solution dyeing — adding colorant to the polymer melt before fiber extrusion — creates fiber where color is an integral part of the structure rather than a surface treatment. Solution-dyed carpet provides the highest colorfastness against all exposure types: sunlight, bleach, ozone, and wet cleaning. It is the standard approach for commercial carpets intended for sunny, outdoor-adjacent, or bleach-cleaned environments.

Yarn-dyed carpet (stock-dyed yarn or skein-dyed yarn) has good colorfastness for standard applications because the dye penetrates the fiber structure, but not as completely as solution dyeing. Piece dyeing — dyeing the finished carpet after tufting — produces good color evenness but typically offers the lowest colorfastness, as the dye sits closer to the fiber surface. Some specialty products use printed patterns, which have varying colorfastness depending on the inks and fiber preparation used.

Reading Colorfastness Ratings

  • AATCC Gray Scale for Color Change: 1–5, where 5 means no perceptible color change and 1 means severe change. Ratings of 4 or 5 are generally considered good performance.
  • Blue Wool Scale for lightfastness: 1–8, where 8 means the best resistance to fading. Commercial specifications often require Blue Wool 5 or above.
  • AATCC Gray Scale for Staining (crocking): 1–5, where 5 means no color transfer. Ratings of 4 or 5 are standard for most commercial applications.
  • Multiple colorfastness ratings may appear on a spec sheet — lightfastness, ozone fastness, and crocking are separate tests with separate values, not a single overall rating.
  • Always check which test standard and conditions the rating was produced under — ratings from different test conditions are not directly comparable.

FAQ

Does solution-dyed fiber improve colorfastness? ⌄

Yes — solution dyeing provides significantly better colorfastness than yarn dyeing or piece dyeing because the color is incorporated into the polymer melt before the fiber is extruded, making it an integral part of the fiber structure rather than a dye absorbed on the surface. Solution-dyed fibers are highly resistant to fading from sunlight, ozone, chlorine bleach, and atmospheric pollutants. This makes solution-dyed carpet the preferred choice for sun-exposed areas, outdoor or semi-outdoor applications, and spaces where bleach disinfection is routine, such as healthcare facilities. The tradeoff is that solution dyeing limits color flexibility — custom or unusual color matching is more difficult with solution-dyed fiber than with yarn-dyed or piece-dyed alternatives.

Is colorfastness affected by cleaning? ⌄

Yes — improper cleaning methods and products can affect the colorfastness of carpet, particularly for yarn-dyed and piece-dyed carpets. Cleaning products with high pH or alkaline formulas can strip dye from fiber surfaces, and products containing bleaching agents can cause visible color loss even when used at low concentrations. High-temperature cleaning or prolonged wet dwell times can also affect dye stability in some fiber systems. Using manufacturer-recommended cleaning products and methods is important both for maintaining colorfastness and for preserving warranty coverage, since improper cleaning can void stain and colorfastness-related warranty protections.

What is crocking and does it affect installed carpet? ⌄

Crocking refers to the transfer of color from carpet fiber to other surfaces through rubbing contact — a dry crocking test measures color transfer under dry conditions, while a wet crocking test measures it when the carpet is wet. Crocking is more commonly tested and more relevant for carpets with surface dye applications or for novelty products with high colorant levels. In practical terms, crocking matters most for carpet tiles or rugs placed on light-colored upholstery, or for carpets in areas where wet contact is common. High crocking ratings indicate color that may rub off onto light-colored clothing or adjacent surfaces, which can be a concern in some installation contexts.

Can high lightfastness prevent all sun fading? ⌄

No — high lightfastness reduces the rate of fading but cannot prevent all color change under prolonged, intense UV exposure. Even solution-dyed fibers will show some degree of color shift after years of direct sun exposure, particularly in very sunny climates or south-facing exposures with unfiltered sunlight. High lightfastness ratings (typically AATCC Gray Scale ratings of 4 or 5, or Blue Wool ratings of 5 or above) indicate that fading under standard test conditions is minimal or imperceptible, but real-world sun exposure varies. UV-filtering window films and window treatments can significantly reduce the fading risk for any carpet in high-sun locations, regardless of colorfastness rating.
