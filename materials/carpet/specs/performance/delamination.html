Carpet Delamination, Explained What it means • How it's reported • Why it matters

Delamination refers to the bond between backing layers. Good delamination resistance keeps the carpet intact under use and cleaning. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Delamination resistance measures the force required to separate carpet backing layers. Higher values generally indicate stronger bonding and better durability.

What it is How it's reported Why it matters FAQ

What it is

Most carpets have multiple backing layers. Delamination resistance describes how strongly those layers are bonded, which affects stability and long-term performance.

How it's reported

Results are typically reported in pounds per inch (lb/in) or newtons. Manufacturers may list minimums or typical ranges based on internal or ASTM test methods.

Why it matters

Low delamination resistance can lead to backing separation, wrinkles, or premature failure—especially in heavy-traffic areas or when aggressive cleaning is used.

FAQ

Is delamination the same as tuft bind? ⌄

No. Tuft bind measures how well yarn stays in the backing, while delamination measures how well backing layers stay bonded.

Can moisture affect delamination? ⌄

Yes. Excess moisture or improper cleaning can weaken latex bonds over time.

Does higher pile weight ensure better delamination? ⌄

Not necessarily. Delamination is more about backing construction and adhesive systems.
