Carpet Flammability, Explained What it means • How it's reported • Why it matters

Flammability ratings indicate how carpet behaves when exposed to fire under standardized tests, often to meet building code requirements. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Flammability refers to a carpet’s reaction to fire, typically measured with tests like ASTM E648/NFPA 253. Results are often reported as critical radiant flux or Class I/II classifications.

What it is How it's reported Why it matters FAQ

What it is

Fire tests assess how readily a floor covering ignites and how it contributes to flame spread. For carpets, the most common method measures critical radiant flux in a controlled environment.

How it's reported

Specs may list critical radiant flux values (watts/cm²) or classes (Class I/II). Higher flux values indicate better fire performance under the test conditions.

Why it matters

Flammability compliance is essential for commercial installations. Results depend on the full system (carpet + cushion + adhesive), so assemblies should be tested together.

FAQ

Is flammability the same as smoke density? ⌄

No. Flammability measures ignition and flame spread, while smoke density measures smoke output.

Do adhesives or cushion affect flammability? ⌄

Yes. Fire testing often requires the full assembly because backing and adhesives can change results.

Are residential carpets tested the same way? ⌄

Not always. Commercial code requirements are typically more stringent and specify particular tests.
