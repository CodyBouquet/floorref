Carpet Gauge Explained Tufting Gauge • Tuft Row Spacing • Why “Tighter” Isn’t the Whole Story

“Gauge” shows up on commercial carpet specs and sometimes on residential tech sheets. It’s easy to think gauge is a straight “quality number,” but it’s really describing a construction dimension: how far apart the tuft rows are. This page explains carpet gauge in plain English and how it relates to density, appearance, and performance. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Carpet gauge (tufting gauge) is the distance between rows of tufts across the carpet, often written as a fraction of an inch (like 1/8 or 1/10). A smaller fraction means the tuft rows are closer together (a “tighter” build). Gauge can support higher density and cleaner pattern definition, but it does not, by itself, define durability.

What Gauge Means

Gauge = tuft row spacing (side-to-side)

Gauge describes the spacing between tuft rows across the width of the carpet. It’s a “side-to-side” spacing measure—how close the rows of yarn are to each other.

Example: a 1/10 gauge carpet has tuft rows spaced closer than a 1/8 gauge carpet.

Gauge is different from stitch rate

Gauge is the side-to-side spacing. Stitch rate is the number of stitches per inch along the length of the carpet (front-to-back direction). Both affect how “tight” a carpet is built, but they’re measuring different directions.

Common Gauges (What You’ll See on Specs)

Gauge (example) What it implies Where you often see it Important note
1/8 gauge Wider tuft row spacing Some styles where texture is more open Can still perform well depending on stitch rate and pile construction
1/10 gauge Tighter row spacing Common in many commercial styles More comparable within similar pile types
1/12 gauge Very tight row spacing Some high-detail patterns / tight loop styles Still not a stand-alone durability score

Not all specs list gauge (especially in many residential broadloom listings). When it’s listed, it’s often because the product is specified for commercial or architect-grade documentation.

What to Pair With Gauge

Gauge + stitch rate = how “tight” the tufting is

If you want a clearer picture of pile tightness, gauge (side-to-side) and stitch rate (front-to-back) belong together. A tighter gauge with a low stitch rate can still be fairly open overall, and vice versa.

Gauge + pile height + face weight = density directionally

Density is about fiber support. Gauge can contribute to density (more rows), but pile height and how much fiber exists (face weight) also matter. Treat gauge as one structural input—not the final verdict.

Gauge matters more for pattern definition than “softness”

A tighter gauge can help patterns look cleaner and more detailed because the tuft rows are closer. Softness is influenced more by fiber type, pile height, and cushion/pad than by gauge alone.

FAQ

Why is gauge written as a fraction (like 1/10)? ⌄

Because it’s a spacing measurement in inches. The fraction indicates the distance between tuft rows. Smaller fractions mean closer spacing (a tighter build).

If a carpet has a tight gauge, will it resist crushing better? ⌄

It can help, because closer rows can contribute to pile support—but crushing resistance also depends on density, fiber resilience, twist (for cut pile), and construction style.

Is gauge relevant for carpet tile? ⌄

Yes—gauge still describes tuft row spacing. But for carpet tile, backing system and dimensional stability are also critical because the floor is made of modular pieces with seams.
