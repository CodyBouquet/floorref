Indentation Resistance Explained Static Load · Residual Indentation · ASTM F1914 · Point Loads

Indentation resistance measures how well a rigid core LVT plank resists permanent denting when concentrated loads are applied to its surface. It is distinct from scratch resistance (which is a wear layer property) and from overall rigidity. A product can be very rigid as a panel while still denting under a point load — or vice versa. The key factor is the core material's density and hardness, not total thickness or wear layer mil. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Indentation resistance is reported as residual indentation depth in mm after a standardized static load test (ASTM F1914 or EN ISO 24343). Lower residual indentation means better resistance. SPC cores (dense, ~1.8–2.0 g/cm³) outperform WPC cores (~0.9–1.2 g/cm³) in indentation resistance. Wear layer thickness does not significantly affect this spec. Furniture protectors and wide-base pads are the most practical mitigation for point-load indentation risk.

What Indentation Resistance Measures

Static load vs. dynamic load

Indentation resistance testing addresses static loads — weight that sits in one place for an extended period. A refrigerator resting on its feet, a filing cabinet, an upright piano, or a bed frame are examples of static loads. This is different from dynamic or rolling loads, which are addressed by separate tests. Static load indentation is the primary concern for rigid core LVT in residential settings because furniture placement is often fixed for months or years. The test measures not the initial compression under load, but the residual — or permanent — depth of the mark after the load is removed and the material has had time to recover.

How the test works

The most commonly referenced US standard is ASTM F1914 (Standard Test Methods for Short-Term Indentation and Residual Indentation of Resilient Floor Coverings Using a Cylindrical Indenter). A cylindrical punch of specified diameter is loaded to 250 pounds and held for a specified time period. After the load is removed, the floor surface is allowed to recover for a defined period, and the remaining depth of the impression — the residual indentation — is measured in millimeters. The European standard EN ISO 24343 follows a similar methodology at different load levels. Lower residual indentation numbers indicate better performance.

Key Factors in Indentation Performance

Core type and density

The rigid core is the primary determinant of indentation resistance in rigid core LVT. SPC (stone polymer composite) cores — with densities of approximately 1.8–2.0 g/cm³ — are significantly harder and denser than WPC (wood polymer composite) cores, which typically measure 0.9–1.2 g/cm³. This density difference directly translates to better indentation resistance in SPC products. Within the SPC category, higher limestone filler content and tighter milling tolerances tend to correlate with better indentation results, though manufacturers do not always publish the filler percentage explicitly.

Backing pad material

The attached underlayment or backing pad affects indentation in a secondary way. A soft EVA foam backing compresses more under a concentrated point load than an IXPE foam backing, which has higher compressive strength. When a stiletto heel or narrow furniture leg applies pressure, the backing's ability to resist compression before transmitting force to the subfloor is part of the total indentation depth. Products with no backing (installed directly on subfloor) often show better indentation resistance than the same core with a thick EVA pad, because there is no compressible layer beneath the core.

Spec Sheet Checklist

  • Look for residual indentation results expressed in millimeters — lower values indicate better performance.
  • Identify the test standard cited (ASTM F1914, EN ISO 24343, or other) to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
  • Consider core type: SPC generally outperforms WPC in indentation resistance due to higher core density.
  • For commercial or heavy-load applications, verify that the residual indentation meets any project specification minimums.
  • Note whether the backing pad (if any) is IXPE or EVA — IXPE compresses less under point loads.
  • Use furniture pads and protectors with wide contact areas regardless of the product's rated performance — this distributes load and is the most reliable mitigation.

FAQ

Does a thicker wear layer improve indentation resistance? ⌄

Not significantly. Indentation resistance is primarily a function of core density and stiffness. The wear layer protects the surface from abrasion and scratches; it does not meaningfully resist point-load indentation because it is thin relative to the core. SPC cores — being denser at approximately 1.8–2.0 g/cm³ — resist indentation better than WPC cores at 0.9–1.2 g/cm³.

Is indentation resistance required for commercial spaces? ⌄

Many commercial project specifications require minimum indentation resistance thresholds. The most common test standards are ASTM F1914 and EN ISO 24343. Commercial-grade products typically report residual indentation of 0.10mm or less under the specified test load. When a commercial specification lists a minimum, it will typically cite the test standard and maximum allowable residual indentation depth in millimeters.

What causes visible dents in LVT floors? ⌄

Visible permanent dents are caused by point loads that concentrate significant force on a small contact area: refrigerator feet, stiletto heels, piano legs, and heavy furniture with narrow tips that lack protective pads. Using floor protectors — wide-base furniture cups or felt pads — distributes the load over a larger area and dramatically reduces indentation risk even for products with moderate indentation resistance.

How is indentation resistance tested? ⌄

The most commonly referenced tests are ASTM F1914 and EN ISO 24343. Both apply a specified load through a standardized indenter for a defined period, then measure the remaining depth of the impression after the load is removed and the material has recovered. The result is reported in millimeters of residual indentation — lower is better. ASTM F1914 uses a 250-pound load applied for one hour; EN ISO 24343-1 uses 20kg/cm² for 24 hours at room temperature.
