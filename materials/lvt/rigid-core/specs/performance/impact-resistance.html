Impact Resistance Explained Drop Tests · Dynamic Load · How It Differs from Dent Resistance

Impact resistance measures how well rigid core LVT withstands damage from objects dropped onto its surface — pots, tools, heavy items that fall rather than rest in place. It is a dynamic property, testing the floor's response to a brief, concentrated energy transfer. This is distinct from indentation resistance, which measures response to sustained static loads. The core material's density, brittleness, and the surface coating's toughness all contribute to impact performance. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Impact resistance describes how the floor handles objects dropped on it — a dynamic, short-duration event. It is tested differently from indentation resistance (static loads) and they should not be conflated. SPC cores are harder than WPC, which affects surface chip and crack resistance from impact. Impact resistance is not universally standardized for LVT and published results should be compared only when using the same test method and conditions.

What Impact Resistance Measures

Dynamic energy absorption

When an object falls onto a floor surface, it transfers kinetic energy to the floor material at the contact point. The floor must either absorb this energy elastically (deforming and recovering), absorb it plastically (deforming permanently), or resist it without deforming. Rigid materials like SPC core can resist more energy before deforming, but when they do deform, the failure mode is typically surface chipping or cracking rather than a smooth dent. WPC cores are slightly more compliant, which can mean a dent rather than a chip when failure occurs — but both are damage modes. The surface coating and wear layer's toughness also play a role in whether impact energy chips or scuffs the visible surface.

How testing is conducted

Impact resistance for LVT is not as uniformly standardized as indentation resistance. Common approaches include drop-weight tests where a steel ball or other standardized mass is dropped from a defined height onto the floor surface, and the surface is then inspected for visible damage (cracking, chipping, delamination). The result is typically a pass/fail at a specified drop height or energy level, or the maximum height at which no visible damage was observed. Some manufacturers reference EN ISO 6603-1 (drop-weight impact for rigid plastics) or ASTM D5420; others publish proprietary test results. Comparing impact resistance data across products requires that the same test method and conditions be used.

Impact Resistance vs. Indentation Resistance

These two specs are frequently confused but measure fundamentally different things. Indentation resistance (ASTM F1914, EN ISO 24343) measures the permanent depth of impression left by a sustained static load applied for an extended period — like a refrigerator foot sitting in place for months. Impact resistance measures the surface's response to a momentary dynamic event — like dropping a heavy pot. A product can have excellent indentation resistance (dense SPC core) while having moderate impact resistance if its surface coating is brittle. Conversely, a product with softer WPC core may absorb impact energy as a smooth dent without chipping, but show more permanent indentation from static furniture loads. Both specs are relevant to real-world use and should be read together.

Spec Sheet Checklist

  • Identify the test method cited — published impact resistance data is only comparable when using the same standard and conditions.
  • Note whether the result is expressed as pass/fail at a specific energy level or as the maximum drop height before damage.
  • Do not confuse impact resistance with indentation resistance — they test different failure modes with different test methods.
  • Consider the core type: SPC cores are harder and more brittle under extreme impact; WPC cores are more compliant but softer under static load.
  • For kitchens, workshops, or high-drop-risk environments, look for impact resistance data alongside surface coating hardness.

FAQ

Does higher impact resistance prevent all surface damage? ⌄

No. Impact resistance test results describe performance under defined test conditions — specific drop heights and standardized impact objects. Real-world impacts with different object shapes, masses, and hardness can still cause damage. Impact resistance is one data point in a broader durability picture that also includes wear layer thickness, surface coating hardness, and core stiffness.

Is impact resistance the same as dent resistance? ⌄

No — they measure different failure modes. Impact resistance describes how the floor responds to a dynamic, falling object — a brief, high-force event. Indentation resistance describes how the floor responds to a sustained static load — heavy furniture sitting in place for months. A product can score well on one while being mediocre on the other. Both specs are relevant and should be read together.

How does the subfloor affect impact resistance? ⌄

Subfloor stiffness affects how impact energy is absorbed. A floating floor with compressible underlayment has slightly more give, which can partially absorb impact energy. Conversely, a floor installed on a soft or flexible subfloor may flex excessively under impact, stressing the core in ways that cause cracking or joint damage. Product testing is typically conducted on a defined rigid substrate, so installed performance may vary.

What test is typically used for LVT impact resistance? ⌄

Impact resistance is not universally standardized for LVT. Some manufacturers reference EN ISO 6603-1 or ASTM D5420; others publish proprietary drop tests or describe performance qualitatively. When published, results are typically reported as pass/fail at a specified condition, or as the maximum drop height at which no visible surface damage was observed. Comparisons are only valid when the same test method and conditions were used.
