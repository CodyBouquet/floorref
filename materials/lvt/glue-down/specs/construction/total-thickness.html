Total Thickness Explained Glue-Down LVT · Floor Height · Subfloor Flatness · Transitions

Total thickness for glue-down LVT is the measurement of all layers combined — the backing, core, print film, wear layer, and surface coating. Unlike rigid-core floating LVT that may reach 8–12 mm or more, glue-down LVT is characteristically thin: most commercial products range from 2 mm to 5 mm total. This thin profile makes glue-down LVT well-suited for renovation work over existing floors and commercial installations where floor height management is critical. Layers that make up total thickness

Glue-down LVT total thickness includes the backing layer (fibrous or PVC, typically 0.5–1 mm), the flexible PVC core (1–2.5 mm), the printed design film (thin, <0.1 mm), and the wear layer (6–28 mil, 0.15–0.71 mm). Unlike rigid-core floating LVT, there is no rigid mineral composite core, no click-lock profile adding edge thickness, and no attached underlayment pad. The result is a structurally thinner assembly that relies entirely on the adhesive bond and subfloor integrity for stability rather than inherent rigidity.

Practical Implications of Thickness

Total thickness affects several practical installation and performance factors. Subfloor telegraphing: thinner products conform more closely to the subfloor, meaning subfloor imperfections are more visible in the finished floor — flatter subfloors reduce this risk. Transition height: total thickness determines the finished floor elevation and the differential at doorways and transitions to adjacent materials. Thinner LVT creates smaller height differentials, useful in renovation work. Indentation resistance: thicker products with denser backings typically show better resistance to point-load indentation from furniture feet and heavy equipment casters. Moisture sensitivity: all glue-down LVT thicknesses require pre-installation moisture testing, but the adhesive system — not the LVT thickness — is the primary moisture-sensitive element.

Spec Sheet Checklist

  • Confirm total thickness in mm and compare with adjacent floor heights to assess transition needs.
  • For renovation projects, add the glue-down LVT thickness to the existing substrate height to determine final floor elevation and door clearances.
  • Check the manufacturer's subfloor flatness requirement for the specific thickness — thinner products typically require flatter subfloors.
  • Note the backing construction (fibrous vs PVC) as this affects adhesive compatibility and dimensional stability.
  • For sound-sensitive applications, do not rely on total thickness alone — verify assembly-level STC and IIC test results.

FAQ

How thick is typical glue-down LVT compared to rigid core? ⌄

Glue-down LVT is generally thinner than rigid-core floating LVT. Glue-down products commonly range from 2 mm to 5 mm total thickness, with 2–3 mm typical for standard commercial products. Rigid core floating LVT typically ranges from 4 mm to 12 mm, with additional thickness from the rigid core and attached underlayment. The thinner profile of glue-down LVT makes it preferred for renovation installations and commercial settings where floor transition height is carefully managed.

Does total thickness affect subfloor flatness requirements for glue-down LVT? ⌄

Yes. Thinner glue-down LVT conforms more closely to the subfloor and telegraphs subfloor irregularities more readily than thicker products. A 2 mm product requires a flatter subfloor than a 4 mm product to avoid visible telegraphing at high spots or adhesive bond failure at low spots. The standard requirement is 3/16 inch over 10 feet, but thinner products may require tighter tolerances per the installation guide.

How does total thickness affect floor-to-floor transitions? ⌄

Total thickness directly determines the finished floor height and the height differential at transitions to adjacent floor materials. Glue-down LVT at 2–3 mm creates a small height difference compared to thicker flooring types. For renovation projects installed over existing flooring, the combined thickness of the existing floor and the glue-down LVT must be assessed against door clearances and transition thresholds.

Does a thicker glue-down LVT product provide better sound performance? ⌄

Thicker glue-down LVT with denser backings provides marginally better sound absorption. However, glue-down LVT bonded directly to concrete slab transmits sound more readily than floating systems with underlayment cushioning — the rigid adhesive bond removes the air gap and compressible layer that floating systems use for IIC improvement. For projects with sound transmission requirements, the complete assembly should be evaluated with tested assembly STC and IIC values.
