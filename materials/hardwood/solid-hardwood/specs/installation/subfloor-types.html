Solid Hardwood Subfloor Types, Explained What it means • How it's reported • Why it matters

Subfloor type determines whether nail-down solid hardwood installation is feasible, what preparation is required, and what grade levels are acceptable. Solid hardwood's requirement for mechanical fastening into a wood-based subfloor is a fundamental installation constraint that limits its use in some building configurations. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Solid hardwood requires a wood subfloor (minimum 3/4 inch plywood or OSB) for nail-down installation. Concrete is not suitable for nail-down. Solid hardwood is not recommended below grade. Flatness must be within 3/16 inch over 10 feet. Grade-level approvals vary by product.

What it is

The subfloor is the structural layer beneath the finish flooring — typically plywood or OSB over joists in wood-frame construction, or concrete in slab construction. Subfloor type determines what installation methods are possible and what preparation is required before hardwood installation can begin.

Subfloor types and their compatibility with solid hardwood:

  • Plywood (APA-rated, Exposure 1 or Exterior): The preferred subfloor for solid hardwood nail-down. Minimum 3/4 inch (19mm) thickness for standard 3/4 inch solid hardwood. Provides excellent fastener withdrawal resistance. Above-grade applications only unless product-specifically approved for on-grade with appropriate moisture barriers.
  • OSB (oriented strand board): Acceptable in most specifications as an alternative to plywood when structurally adequate and properly fastened. OSB has lower fastener withdrawal resistance than plywood, particularly with moisture exposure (edges can swell and delaminate). Minimum 3/4 inch for solid hardwood nail-down.
  • Concrete: Not suitable for standard nail-down. Some products allow direct glue-down over on-grade concrete with strict moisture limits. Most commonly, a wood subfloor (plywood over sleepers or raised subfloor system) must be installed over concrete before solid hardwood.
  • Existing wood flooring (overlay): Acceptable as subfloor when structurally sound, flat, and securely fastened — subject to height restrictions. New solid hardwood must be installed at 45-degree or perpendicular angle to existing floor direction.

How it's reported

Manufacturer installation guides list approved subfloor types, minimum thickness requirements, flatness tolerances, and grade-level approvals (above-grade, on-grade, below-grade). NWFA's Installation Guide provides subfloor requirements organized by installation method and subfloor material, and is the reference standard when manufacturer guides are silent on specific conditions.

Subfloor preparation requirements — fastening of loose areas, grinding of high spots, patching of low spots, moisture barrier application — are also addressed in installation documentation. Subfloor flatness is measured with a long straightedge or laser level. Any areas out of tolerance must be corrected before flooring installation begins, not after.

Why it matters

Subfloor type is a fundamental constraint in solid hardwood specification — not a detail to be resolved during installation. In buildings with concrete slab construction (common in multifamily, commercial, and slab-on-grade residential), nail-down solid hardwood cannot be installed without adding a wood subfloor system first. This adds cost, raises the finished floor height, and requires coordination with adjacent floor transitions and door clearances.

Subfloor flatness directly affects the finished floor appearance and performance. A solid hardwood floor installed over an uneven subfloor will mirror that unevenness — boards will rock, hollow sounds will develop underfoot, and fasteners will experience concentrated stress at board edges. Correcting subfloor problems before installation is far less costly than attempting remediation after the floor is installed.

For renovation projects, the existing subfloor condition should be evaluated as early as possible in the project timeline. Subfloor repairs — refastening, patching, leveling, or replacement — can affect project scheduling significantly. Discovering subfloor problems during flooring installation delays the project and can create conflicts with other trades working on tight schedules.

FAQ

What subfloor is required for solid hardwood nail-down installation? ⌄

Nail-down solid hardwood requires a wood-based subfloor — plywood or OSB — of sufficient thickness to provide fastener holding power and structural stability. NWFA guidelines specify a minimum 3/4 inch (19mm) plywood or OSB subfloor for nail-down installation of standard 3/4 inch solid hardwood. The subfloor must be structurally sound, adequately fastened to the joists, and within flatness tolerance. Plywood (APA-rated, Exposure 1) is preferred by many installers because it provides higher screw and fastener withdrawal resistance than OSB and is less susceptible to edge swell from moisture exposure during construction.

Can solid hardwood be installed below grade or over concrete? ⌄

Solid hardwood is not recommended for below-grade installations (basements) because below-grade concrete and surrounding soil present variable, often high moisture conditions that exceed solid hardwood's tolerance. At or on-grade, solid hardwood may be permitted in some product specifications with a wood subfloor (plywood over sleepers or a raised system) to separate the hardwood from the concrete. Direct adhesive installation of solid hardwood over on-grade concrete is allowed by some manufacturers only when moisture vapor emission rates are within strict tested limits. Confirming the specific product's grade-level approvals against the actual installation conditions is required before specifying solid hardwood below or on grade.

What flatness tolerance is required for subfloors under solid hardwood? ⌄

NWFA requires that the subfloor surface be flat within 3/16 inch over a 10-foot span, and 1/8 inch over a 6-foot span, before solid hardwood installation. High spots cause boards to rock and create hollow areas prone to movement noise. Low spots allow boards to flex under foot traffic. Both conditions create fastener stress and can accelerate joint failure. Subfloor flatness corrections — grinding high spots on concrete, filling low spots with floor patching compounds, adding shims under plywood — must be completed and allowed to cure before installation begins.

Can solid hardwood be installed over existing wood flooring? ⌄

Yes, in some cases. Installing solid hardwood over an existing wood floor (as an overlay) is possible if the existing floor is structurally sound, securely fastened, flat within tolerance, and the combined thickness does not create door clearance or transition height problems. The existing floor must be inspected for loose boards, squeaks, and moisture damage before use as a subfloor. The new solid hardwood is typically installed at 45 degrees or perpendicular to the existing floor direction to prevent joint alignment. Total assembly height — existing floor thickness plus new hardwood thickness — must be checked against all door swings, thresholds, and adjacent floor transitions before proceeding.
