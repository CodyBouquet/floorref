Engineered Hardwood Subfloor Types, Explained What it means · How it's reported · Why it matters

Subfloor type refers to the surface the engineered hardwood is installed over. Unlike solid hardwood, engineered hardwood can be installed over concrete, wood panel subfloors, and in some cases existing resilient flooring — but each subfloor type imposes specific requirements for flatness, moisture, and installation method. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Engineered hardwood is compatible with concrete (above-grade and on-grade), plywood, OSB, and some existing resilient flooring. Each subfloor type has specific requirements for flatness (typically 3/16" per 10'), moisture limits, and which installation methods are permitted.

What it is

Common subfloor types approved for engineered hardwood and their key requirements:

  • Concrete slab (above-grade / on-grade): Suitable for floating and glue-down installation methods. Requires moisture testing (calcium chloride or in-situ RH probe) before installation. Must meet flatness tolerance and be free of contamination, curing compounds, and sealers that would interfere with adhesive bond.
  • Concrete slab (below-grade): Only permitted for products specifically approved for below-grade use. Higher moisture risk requires moisture barrier systems. Not all engineered hardwood is rated for below-grade installation.
  • Plywood: Suitable for floating, glue-down, and nail-down/staple-down methods. Minimum thickness requirements vary by method — nail-down typically requires minimum 3/4 inch plywood of appropriate grade. Moisture testing with a pin meter is required.
  • OSB (oriented strand board): Generally acceptable for floating and glue-down; some manufacturers restrict nail-down over OSB. OSB has lower moisture resistance than plywood and requires careful moisture management.
  • Existing resilient flooring: Some products approve floating (and occasionally glue-down) over existing vinyl tile or sheet vinyl, provided the existing floor is firmly bonded, in good condition, flat within tolerances, and free of asbestos-containing material.

How it's reported

Installation guides list approved subfloor types by material and grade, often organized by installation method. The guide specifies minimum subfloor thickness, flatness tolerance, surface preparation requirements, and moisture limits for each approved subfloor type. Restrictions (such as "not for below-grade installation" or "not for installation over OSB with nail-down method") are equally important to note.

The flatness tolerance is typically expressed as a maximum variation in elevation over a specified span — commonly 3/16 inch in 10 feet (approximately 5mm per 3 meters). High spots must be corrected by grinding or planing; low spots are filled with approved patching or self-leveling compounds. The flatness requirement is a pre-installation condition, not something that can be compensated for with underlayment alone.

Why it matters

The subfloor is the foundation of the flooring system. An inadequate subfloor causes problems that cannot be corrected after the floor is installed — a subfloor that is too wet causes moisture damage; one that is too uneven causes joint stress, hollow spots, and rocking boards; one that is too thin for nail-down installation causes squeaking and fastener pullout. Subfloor assessment is the most important pre-installation step because discovering problems after the floor is down requires full removal to correct.

The ability to install engineered hardwood over concrete — which solid hardwood generally cannot accommodate without a sleeper system — is one of the main reasons engineered products are selected for slab-on-grade and below-grade applications. This versatility is a key product attribute, but it comes with the corresponding responsibility to verify moisture conditions in the slab, which are often more variable and challenging than wood subfloor conditions.

Existing subfloors in renovation projects may also contain hazardous materials — particularly pre-1980 resilient flooring and flooring adhesives that may contain asbestos. Any existing flooring or adhesive suspected of containing asbestos must be tested and handled according to local regulations before any disturbance or new flooring installation over it proceeds.

FAQ

What subfloor types are acceptable for engineered hardwood installation? ⌄

Most engineered hardwood can be installed over concrete slabs (above-grade and on-grade), plywood, OSB, and existing resilient flooring for floating installations. Below-grade concrete is only permitted for products specifically approved for that use. The specific subfloor requirements depend on the installation method — not all subfloor and method combinations are approved for every product. Always verify in the product's installation guide.

What flatness tolerance is required for engineered hardwood subfloors? ⌄

Most manufacturers specify a maximum variation of 3/16 inch per 10 feet (approximately 5mm per 3 meters). Some products require a tighter 3/16 inch per 6 feet. High spots must be ground down; low spots must be filled with approved leveling compound. Out-of-flat subfloors cause hollow spots in glue-down installations, joint stress in floating installations, and visible rocking in the installed floor. Flatness is different from levelness — a sloped but flat subfloor is generally acceptable.

Can engineered hardwood be installed over concrete below grade? ⌄

Some products are approved for below-grade concrete installation; others are restricted to on-grade or above-grade only. Below-grade slabs have higher risk of sustained elevated moisture — ground moisture can migrate through concrete indefinitely. Products approved for below-grade use must be paired with appropriate moisture barrier systems. Using a product not rated for below-grade installation in a basement is a common warranty void condition for moisture-related failures.

Can engineered hardwood be installed over radiant heat subfloors? ⌄

Many — but not all — engineered hardwood products are approved for installation over radiant heat systems. The product must be specifically rated for radiant heat use; approval depends on core construction, veneer species, and adhesive system. Radiant heat surface temperature must remain within the floor's rated maximum (commonly 80–85°F at the floor surface), and the system must be operated at reduced temperatures during the first weeks after installation. The radiant heat approval is stated in the installation guide and is a warranty compliance condition.
