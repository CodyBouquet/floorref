Engineered Hardwood Moisture Testing, Explained What it means · How it's reported · Why it matters

Moisture testing is the pre-installation measurement of subfloor moisture content or vapor emission to verify that conditions are within the limits specified for the flooring system. Over concrete slabs, it is typically a mandatory step before any wood-based flooring can be installed. Reference-only: no product recommendations.

Quick answer

Moisture testing is required before installing engineered hardwood — especially over concrete. Calcium chloride (ASTM F1869) and in-situ RH probe (ASTM F2170) are the standard test methods. Results must fall within the limits specified in the product's installation guide for the warranty to be valid.

What it is

Two standard test methods are used for moisture testing over concrete subfloors:

  • Calcium chloride test (ASTM F1869): A sealed chamber containing anhydrous calcium chloride crystals is placed on the concrete surface for 60–72 hours. The weight gain of the crystals indicates the moisture vapor emission rate (MVER) in pounds per 1,000 square feet per 24 hours. This is a surface-level measurement and reflects conditions at the time of testing.
  • In-situ relative humidity probe (ASTM F2170): A probe is inserted into a drilled hole at 40% of the slab depth and sealed for at least 72 hours to equilibrate. The RH reading reflects the long-term moisture equilibrium deep within the slab — considered a more reliable predictor of long-term performance than the calcium chloride method, which only measures surface emissions.
  • Wood subfloor moisture meter: Pin-type or pinless meters measure moisture content (MC) of wood subfloors in percentage, which is then compared against the acclimated flooring MC to check for acceptable differential.

How it's reported

Manufacturers specify moisture limits in their installation guides, often separately for each test method. Common formats include "maximum 3 lbs per 1,000 sq ft per 24 hours (ASTM F1869)" or "maximum 80% RH (ASTM F2170)." Some installation guides accept both methods; others require one method specifically. When a moisture barrier adhesive is used, the installation guide may specify higher allowable moisture limits based on the adhesive's vapor barrier capability.

Test results should be documented — date, location within the slab, test method, and result — before installation begins. This documentation serves as evidence of compliance in the event of a future moisture-related warranty claim. The installation guide often explicitly states that failure to test and document moisture conditions prior to installation voids moisture-related warranty coverage.

Why it matters

Elevated subfloor moisture is the single most common cause of post-installation failure in wood-based flooring over concrete. Moisture vapor rising from a slab that exceeds the flooring system's tolerance causes the wood to absorb moisture, swell, cup, and — in severe cases — completely delaminate from the subfloor. These failures are costly: correction typically requires full floor removal, slab remediation, and reinstallation. Moisture testing before installation allows any out-of-range conditions to be addressed before the floor goes down.

Concrete slabs in new construction require particular attention — concrete can retain significant moisture for 60–90 days or more after pouring, and construction timelines often push flooring installation before the slab is sufficiently dry. In below-grade and on-grade installations, ongoing groundwater vapor transmission means some slabs may never dry below limits without a separate moisture control system.

The decision to proceed despite elevated moisture readings — by installing a separate moisture membrane or using a high-tolerance moisture barrier adhesive — should only be made if the chosen system's documented vapor permeance matches the actual test results. There is no single "safe" threshold that applies universally; each flooring and adhesive system has its own specification.

FAQ

What moisture test methods are used for engineered hardwood installation? ⌄

Two primary methods are used over concrete: calcium chloride testing (ASTM F1869), which measures surface moisture vapor emission rate over 60–72 hours; and in-situ relative humidity probes (ASTM F2170), which measure RH deep within the slab for a more predictive result. Wood subfloors are tested with a pin-type or pinless moisture meter to measure moisture content percentage. The manufacturer's installation guide specifies which test method is required and the maximum acceptable result.

Why is subfloor moisture testing required before installing engineered hardwood? ⌄

Excessive subfloor moisture — especially from concrete slabs — is one of the leading causes of flooring failure. Moisture vapor rising from concrete can cause cupping, warping, and adhesive bond failure. Testing verifies the slab's emission rate is within the tolerance the flooring system can handle. Documentation of test results is typically required for warranty claims on moisture-related failures; skipping the test voids moisture coverage in most manufacturer warranties.

What are acceptable moisture levels for installation over concrete? ⌄

Acceptable limits vary by product and adhesive system — always verify in the specific manufacturer's installation guide. Common reference limits for calcium chloride testing are 3–5 lbs/1,000 sq ft/24 hours; for in-situ RH probes, typical limits range from 75–85% RH. If the slab tests above the specified limit, installation should not proceed until the slab is remediated or an appropriate moisture barrier system is added.

Does moisture testing apply to wood subfloors as well as concrete? ⌄

Yes. Wood subfloors are tested with a moisture meter; acceptable moisture content is typically 6–9% MC, and the differential between the subfloor and acclimated flooring boards should generally not exceed 2–4 percentage points. Wood subfloors over crawl spaces or below-grade areas are particularly prone to elevated moisture and may require additional moisture management before installation can proceed.
